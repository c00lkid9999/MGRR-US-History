The Watergate Scandal

Submission 3: Timeline of Watergate

Pre-Scandal

January 1969: Richard Nixon is inaugurated as the 37th president of the United States, following Lyndon B. Johnson. He served as president until 1974.

February 1971: Nixon orders the installation of a secret taping system. This records all conversations in the Oval Office, his Executive Office Building office, and his Camp David office, along with selected telephones in these locations.

June 1971: The Pentagon Papers, an interior secret history of the Vietnam War, are published by the New York Times, and later, the Washington Post. Nixon recruits a team to investigate this leak, attempting to incriminate the defense analyst who leaked the papers to the press.

January 1972: One of the members of the committee that investigated the Pentagon Papers leak is transferred to the Committee to Re-Elect the President. He creates a plan for espionage against the Democratic Party.

Major Events

June 1972: In this infamous event, five men are arrested after breaking into the Democratic National Committee Headquarters. Found with them are bugging devices, cash, and rolls of film. The White House denies involvement in the break-in. Reporters begin to call in to learn more about this event, filing stories about it as they learn information.

Remainder of 1972: Reporters continue to investigate this event, discovering many things: a check for $25,000 marked for Nixon’s reelection campaign was deposited into the account of one of the criminals arrested for the break-in, Nixon’s Attorney General had controlled a secret fund to finance intelligence gathering against democrats, and the FBI had made connections between Nixon’s aides and the break-in.

January 1973: The Watergate break-in trial begins. Many people involved in the break-in are convicted.

March 1973: Nixon tries to cover up the scandal, however, Watergate burglar James McCord confesses the existence of a wider conspiracy, starting to unravel the cover-up.

April 1973: Many people close to Nixon begin to resign, including the acting FBI director and two White House aides. White House counsel John Dean is fired. Nixon delivers his first address on Watergate, trying to show himself as innocent.

May 1973: The first nationally televised hearings of the Senate Select Committee begin.

July to October 1973: Nixon disconnects the taping system in the White House. He refuses to turn over recordings of the White House conversations to the investigation, which were believed to include important evidence for the case.

August 1973: The Senate Select Committee wraps up the hearings, and Nixon delivers another primetime address, saying that he is being formed through the hearings and commentary on Watergate.

October 1973: Gerald Ford becomes vice president, Nixon fires Archibald Cox in the “Saturday Night Massacre.” Nixon’s attorney general and Deputy Attorney resign, rather than carrying out this order.

November 1973: Nixon famously declares “I’m not a crook,” continuing to proclaim his innocence.

End of the Scandal

April 1974: Transcripts of the Nixon tapes are released by the White House.

May 1974: Impeachment proceedings against Nixon are started.

July 1974: The Supreme Court rules that Nixon must surrender dozens of original tape recordings. Articles of impeachment against Nixon are approved by the House Judiciary Committee and sent to the House for a vote, but the vote was never carried out.

August 1974: The “smoking gun,” conversation transcripts revealing Nixon’s involvement in the Watergate cover-up, are released by Nixon. President Nixon resigns shortly afterward to avoid being impeached. Gerald Ford becomes president.

September 1974: Gerald Ford pardons Nixon.

Submission 4: Mini Essay on Watergate

Watergate was a major event in the decade of the 1970s. It was the first and only time that a president has resigned in all of United States history, a dramatic culmination to a series of scandals that shocked the nation. Because of its impact on social life and the high-level base of its impact, Watergate was the most important political event of the 1970s. Firstly, Watergate had a huge impact on social life. Initially, election survey data in 1972, before Watergate, showed that Nixon best reflected moral standards more than other candidates, and 74.5% of respondents believed him to be a trustworthy president. However, the events of Watergate upturned society. Many people, especially former voters for Nixon, were less trusting of people and politicians after the events of Watergate, and trust in the press dropped for Nixon voters while being raised for McGovern voters, with the reverse happening for television media. This shows how Watergate opened rifts in society due to political disagreements, meaning that it had a major social impact. Second, Watergate had a huge impact due to the incredibly high-level nature of the scandal. At times, the scandal led people to believe democracy as a whole was at stake, not just Nixon as the president, especially in cases such as the “Saturday Night Massacre,” where Nixon fired many top leaders while trying to find someone who would fire the prosecutor against him. This shows how the political impact of Watergate was high, as people were worried that United States democracy would not be able to withstand it. In all, Watergate was an important political event, and it had many impacts. Because of those impacts on social life and the high-level nature of the scandal, Watergate was the most important political event of the 1970s.
